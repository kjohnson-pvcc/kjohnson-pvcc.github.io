U.S. TITLE 26 - INTERNAL REVENUE CODE

The Internal Revenue Code is a body of laws that arrange all laws pertaining to federal, income, estate, gift, alcohol, tobacco, and employment taxes for the United States.

ORGIN

    Prior to 1874 U.S. laws were not organized by subject matter, thus began the codification of these laws. The Internal Revenue Code or IRC for short was reviewed in 1939 and officially put into place. The IRC laws were re-examined and reviewed in 1954 and again in 1986. Because of the many changes made during the Tax Reform Act of 1986, the IRC is know as the “Internal Revenue Code of 1986”.

THE IRS and U.S. DEPARTMENT OF THE TREASURY

    The Internal Revenue Service (IRS) execute these laws through the Treasury Regulations and Revenue Rulings. It is the Internal Revenue Service’s responsibility to enforce the internal revenue tax laws based on the Internal Revenue Code Section 7801.
    The tax policy develops and implements programs, tax policies, reviews rulings and regulations for the Internal Revenue Code.

ORGANIZATION

    The Internal Revenue Code is broken into eleven sections subtitle sections. These subtitle sections are broken down into chapters, which are broken down into subchapters. The subtitle sections are listed below.
    Subtitle A – Income Taxes
    Subtitle B – Estate and Gift Taxes
    Subtitle C – Employment Taxes
    Subtitle D – Miscellaneous Excise Taxes
    Subtitle E – Alcohol, Tobacco, and Certain Other Excises Taxes
    Subtitle F – Procedure and Administration
    Subtitle G – The Joint Committee on Taxation
    Subtitle H – Financing of Presidential Election Campaigns
    Subtitle I – Trust Fund Code
    Subtitle J – Coal Industry Health Benefits
    Subtitle K – Group Health Plan Requirements

CODE 26 & THE CENSUS

    The U.S Census Bureau uses data collected from the IRS about households and businesses. Title 26 allows the IRS to disclose Federal Tax Returns and Return Information (FTI) to other agencies, including the Census Bureau. Title 26 U.S. Code 6103 specifically, allows the IRS to share FTI with the Census Bureau for statistical work related to national economic purposes authorized by law.

SUBTITLE A - CHAPTER 1

    The most commonly know or used tax law is under Subtitle A Chapter 1 Subchapter B, Computation of Taxable Income. This subchapter breaks down what the definition of gross income, adjusted gross income, and taxable income and how to compute it. Part III of Subchapter B goes into detail of what is specifically excluded from gross income, while Part IV through VII break down what deductions and exemptions are allowed from any income earned. Gross income is any income from services, including fees, commissions, dividends, royalties, etc. Adjusted gross income is any income minus any deductions allowed from section 62. Taxable income is any gross income minus any deductions allowed from section 63.
